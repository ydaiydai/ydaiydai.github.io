Borderlands 3:
Why It is a Failure

While the game has had a commercial success,
Borderlands 3 will fail to have a long-lasting great legacy.

It has been more than a year since Borderlands 3’s initial release in September 2019. By February 2020, it has sold nearly 8 million copies and it is that this game has been a commercial success. However, as my title suggests, Borderlands 3 is nowhere near as successful as its direct predecessor: Borderlands 2. From fundamental game design to its commercial strategy, Borderlands 3 fails to continue its precursor’s long-lasting vigor.

Players usually describe the Borderlands series as looter-shooters; in fact, Borderlands 1 is often considered as the first looter-shooter with its “gazillions” of guns. After that, games like Destiny and The Division followed Borderlands’ footsteps into this new and thriving genre. Like any other looter game, the most important focus of looter-shooter developers is replayability. Replaying the game many times, creating different powerful characters, and grinding for more randomized loots are what any looter is about. Borderlands 3 failed at this very aspect. To encourage players to go back to the same stories and main campaign, the game must avoid being too long and tedious. Looters especially looter-shooters should not chase a game length of a single-player story-telling RPG. For comparison, based on my more than 10 playthroughs of Borderlands 2, the main campaign can take about 10-20 hours to complete; Borderlands 3 on the other hand, would take more than 30 hours according to gameinformer and I can confirm this statement. Although players can always find ways to speedrun the game, for average players, playing two playthroughs of Borderlands 3 would take roughly the time to play Borderlands 2 four times. Getting half the result with twice the effort discourages players greatly. Its great graphics and gameplay are being overshadowed by long and tedious playthrough.

Another major flaw in Borderlands 3’s game design is its management of loots. A looter cannot be a looter without its loots and Borderlands 3 has plenty of them, too much in fact. Anyone who has enjoyed Borderlands 2 would agree that obtaining legendary gears in that game is not an easy task; a player has to go through a boss or a particular enemy many times to just have one drop of a legendary gear. It is the opposite in Borderlands 3 as the player will have countless of drops of rare gears in a single run. Some people might argue that the loot drop mechanics in Borderlands 3 is better since the player can obtain what they desire easier, but they miss the important point: A thing is precious when it is rare. The intrigue of getting loots is the excitement of getting the gear that you have struggled for hours and it is not the constant stimulus of “orange” (the color of legendary gears in Borderlands series) dropping. If the character’s inventory gets filled quickly by all of the best gears, the gears lose their significance. Borderlands 3's additional rarity layer of “anointment” on gears does add some worth to loot grinding, but it will only attract the most hardcore gamers since most casual players are more interested in unique legendary gears rather than their specific stats and perks. Together with the its long game length and a rather controversial main story (the story can be a whole new discussion), Borderlands 3 is quickly losing player base. According to Steam Charts, Borderlands 2 has been able to maintain a consistent 5000 daily active player-base more than eight years after its launch while Borderlands 3 has dropped to a similar number just one year after its initial release. Longevity is crucial for looter-shooters and Borderlands 3 is failing on this aspect miserably.

Again, some people defending the game might suggest that the small player base on steam is caused by the splitting of players on PC platform. Statistically, I agree with the argument, but even with the assumption that the other platforms such Epic Games have the same player numbers, the state of Borderlands 3’s player base is still far from optimal. On the topic of different platforms on PC, it can be argued that this has hurt the franchise in some ways. While Gearbox has claimed that Borderlands 3 has sold well on Epic Games, the company’s decision of making the game exclusive on one PC platform for half a year has divided its player base. Some fans on steam ultimately refuse the buy the game, and many others can never see the game under a positive light though they eventually purchase the game; the rather unimpressive reviews on steam can reflect this. The bad PR relation continues to hurt the game’s reputation as DLCs and other additional contents have been blasted with negative reviews. Although the commercial motivation of having exclusivity contract is understandable, the move inevitably disrupted the momentum of launch and stifled many gamers’ enthusiasm. Just recently, Borderlands 3 released its “Season Pass 2”. This kind of anti-consumer practice has been used before in Borderlands 2, but it is much worse this time; after the exclusivity deal followed by poor pricings and bad qualities of the game’s DLCs, this blatantly greedy move has further damaged Gearbox’s relation to a level of irreparable. This greed of trying to squeeze as many pennies as possible from consumers is quickly making the game run out of steam.

The poor design in game length and implementation of loot mechanics along with Gearbox’s shameless anti-consumer practices have made Borderlands 3 a flagrant letdown. While hurting the franchise’s reputation, Gearbox’s decision to gain more profit will ultimately not lead to commercial success as the game has been quickly losing its longevity, Borderlands 3 will forever live under the shadow of its glorious predecessor.

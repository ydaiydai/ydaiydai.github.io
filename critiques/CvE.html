Created vs. Established

Which is better in an RPG:
A created protagonist or an established protagonist?
It depends.

We can divide playable protagonist in single-player RPGs into two categories: Created Protagonist and Established (non-creatable) Protagonist. Having a created protagonist means that the player would usually personally customize a character based on his/her own preferences, the player can choose the character’s gender, body type, personality, and so on; the typical examples of games with character creations are the Fallout and The Elder Scrolls series. On the other hand, having an established protagonist means that the player can only play as one or a few characters who have been created and established in the world setting with their own backgrounds, the player usually has very limited customization options; a few examples are the Witcher series and most of the single-player JRPGs. How a protagonist is implemented seems arbitrary, but there is a connection between protagonist creation and the main focus of a game.

Though outliers and unique designs always exist, the general pattern for the connection mentioned is that if an RPG (especially open-world) values its sandbox elements (allows the player a great degree of creativity) more, the game would normally allow the player to create his/her own character; if an RPG values its stories more, the game would usually give the player its own established character. Many great games can value both aspects equally and have their owns implementation of character creation (Baldur’s Gate 3 for example), but in most cases, the pattern described is a secret of an RPG’s success.

If you are quite clear on what I am suggesting, check out Cyberpunk 2077. As the most anticipated game in 2020, Cyberpunk 2077 ended up being a disappointment. While a big reason is CD Projekt Red’s deceiving marketing on last-gen consoles which triggered a massive backlash from console players, PC players are not happy either. Even if we ignore the problem of bugs (which is a big problem), we can see that Cyberpunk 2077 has struggles greatly with its immersion. In the game, the player plays as his/her own customized V and many gamers have failed to build a connection with the main character in Cyberpunk’s story-rich sci-fi world. Judging from the game’s lifeless city and its horrendous AI system, it is clear that CD Projekt Red did not aim to build a sophisticated sandbox, they focused on the game’s campaign. The game with its character creation failed to either engage the player with its great stories or give the player a fun sandbox. The reason why custom character is not as impactful as a pre-built character is simple: the custom character is a stranger. Movies and novels are engaging not because readers and watchers can have their own custom characters but because they can read and watch interesting stories of others. The Witcher series is so successful because the main character Geralt of Rivia is well established (just imagine if every Witcher game lets you make your own character). CD Projekt Red, tried new ways developing Cyberpunk 2077 after the Witcher series (Witcher 3 screenshot above) but failed to make the new ways work. In addition, Cyberpunk 2077 only allows first-person perspective in game and it makes the player even more difficult to establish a connection with the protagonist. The game seldom shows the protagonist (unless through a mirror or while driving). When you cannot even see your character in game, why should you even bother spending so much time customizing.

Some games, however, might work better if there were character creation. One example is Kingdom Come: Deliverance. In the game, you play as Henry (as shown from the image on the left), the son of a blacksmith, and your story begins after your village was raided and your parents were slaughtered. The game is widely believed to have taken inspiration from The Elder Scrolls series, the paradigm of modern RPGs with character creation. But unlike The Elder Scrolls, Kingdom Come: Deliverance does not have any character creation, every Henry looks the same; and while the game has a lot to offer with its perks system, it lacks variety in the aspect of character’s physical look. The protagonist’s lack of background story gives the developers the room for character customization. Though the game’s setting is not fantastical like The Elder Scrolls, having a character creation option might lift Kingdom Come: Deliverance to a new level.

Using pre-built character in a single-player RPG is generally a safer choice for game developers. While it might limit the player’s level of creativity, it usually makes the game more cohesive. Unless the game strongly emphasizes its sandbox elements or has much room for a created protagonist, giving the player established characters will only lead to few criticisms if at all. Having character creation sometimes is just icing on the cake. This is deep topic and many gamers will have their own opinions, but most players, having experienced different games, would agree with the article’s assessment on values of having created character or established character in a game. But after all, each game is different and requires much deliberation when figuring out whether a character creation is beneficial or not.
